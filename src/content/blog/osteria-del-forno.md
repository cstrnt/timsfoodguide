There's a version of this restaurant that exists in every city that takes itself too seriously. The dough fermented for exactly seventy-two hours, the tomatoes sourced from a single farm on Vesuvius, the menu so pared back it's practically a manifesto. Osteria del Forno flirts with that energy without tipping into it, which is exactly the right call.

The room is long and narrow, with a wood-burning oven at the back that you can see from almost every table. It gets warm. In summer this is probably a problem; in March it felt like the point.

## The pizza

The margherita is the place to start, as it always is. The crust has that right amount of char, properly leoparded, with a cornicione that puffs without being empty. The fior di latte is applied with restraint — this isn't a cheese pizza with tomato, it's a tomato pizza with cheese, which matters more than it sounds. They finish it with a few leaves of fresh basil that wilt just enough in the residual heat.

I also tried the diavola, which leans on 'nduja rather than ordinary salami. The fat renders and pools slightly and makes the whole thing richer and more interesting. The spice is real but not a stunt.

## Everything else

The antipasti are solid workhorses: burrata with good oil, a board of cured meat that doesn't try to be clever. The wine list is short, Italian, and priced fairly. The house red is unidentified but pleasant.

## Notes

Service is warm and efficient, which in this city is not as common as it should be. The noise level at full house is high — this is not the place for a conversation that requires concentration. Book ahead on weekends.

For what it is, it delivers reliably and without pretension. That's enough.
